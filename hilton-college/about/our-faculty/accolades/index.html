Faculty Accolades

  • Drs. Agnes DeFranco and Jason Draper have received a publication acceptance notification from Tourism Review International for their article entitled "Travelers’ perceptions of cultural, economic, and environmental impacts on travel decisions: Does household income and job security matter?" Using the theory of case-based planning, this study examines if travelers’ perceptions of their own cultural, economic and environmental travel experiences impact their decision to travel domestically and internationally.
  • Dr. Priyanko Guchait has received a publication acceptance notification from the International Journal of Hospitality and Tourism Administration for his article entitled " The Impact of Workplace Multi-foci Information on Error Tolerance: Roles of Organizational Culture and Leadership Style." He studies the social information processing model to examine how organizational culture and leadership, as two major informational sources, jointly influence employees’ perceived error tolerance and job satisfaction.
  • Drs. They have also received a publication acceptance notification from the Hospitality Financials and Technology Professionals (HFTP) Technology and Finance Hospitality Summer 2024 for their article entitled " Corporate Digital Responsibility: Non-negotiable in the Digital Age."
  • Dr. Chris Taylor and Ph.D. Student Kristen Rinck have received a publication acceptance notification from the Journal of Wine Research for their article entitled "Uncorking Organizational Identity: Exploring Identity and Identity Threat Responses among Sommeliers." The researchexplores the identities of Court of Master Sommeliers members in light of recent incidents, using qualitative research and Petriglieri’s (2011) identity threat theoretical framework. The findings indicate that the strength of an identity and experience play a large role in how members respond to identity threats within the organization.
  • Drs. Mary Dawson and Juan Madera have received a publication acceptance notification from the Journal of Hospitality & Tourism Management for their article entitled "Think leader, think man? The role of implicit bias, status-legitimizing beliefs, and gender in hospitality leadership discrimination." The research investigates how social norms influence perceptions of women in leadership by examining perceptions of reverse discrimination and gender hierarchies. Results indicated implicit bias increased perceptions of reverse discrimination.
  • Dr. Mohamed Mohamed has received the New Faculty Research Grant from the University of Houston, for his article entitled "Challenges, Preferences, and Adaptation Strategies of Older Adults Using Technology-Enabled Services in Hospitality and Tourism." This abstract will dive into the characterization that older adults with high leisure time and disposable income are vastly growing in the tourism sector. This demographic faces unique challenges though, in terms of barriers to technology usage and are seeking inclusivity which will enhance their quality of life.
  • Dr. Carl Boger has received the New Faculty Research Grant from the Journal of Human Resources in Hospitality and Tourism, for his paper entitled "Designing a mentorship program for women vs. men." The study examines the impact of mentorship program quality and design on perceived organizational support between female versus male applicants in the Middle East hospitality sector. Theoretical implications include that a resource must be perceived as quality and the design of the resource impacts the perception of POS. Practically, designing a mentorship program toward women makes them feel more supportive.
  • Drs. Minjung Shin, Ki-Joon Bach and Ph.D. Student, Yunna Park have received a publication acceptance notification from the Journal of Hospitality and Tourism Management for their research entitled, for their research entitled "Effectively Implementing Technology in Heritage Hotels: Balancing Tradition and Technology." The research explored how technology has become essential in the hospitality industry when meeting changing customer expectations, improving operations and enhancing service. The study explored the disruptions of immersive technology and expands on fluency theory. It also details that brand identity maintains customer immersion and protects brand value. 
  • Dr. Carl Boger has received the New Faculty Research Grant from the Journal of Hospitality & Tourism Education, for his paper entitled "Importance-Performance Analysis: Explaining the Lack of Minority Faculty members." In comparison to the racial composition of undergraduate populations, the hospitality discipline lacks underrepresented minority (UM) faculty members. This study explored causes for this phenomenon. Findings underscore the importance of hospitality programs intentionally mentoring UMs for academic roles, enhancing research-focused curricula, providing necessary UM support, fostering inclusive environments, and encouraging UM student-faculty interactions. Notably, the lack of attention to these factors adversely affects the presence of UM faculty members in the hospitality discipline.

The Moderating Role of Food Cultural Proximity." The results indicate that when international tourists' self-congruity with a destination cousine is increased. they develop more positive attitudes toward both local food and an affinity to the destination. 
  • Dr. Kyle Hight has received the New Faculty Research Program grant from University of Houston for his research entitled "The impact of employer payment frequency on restaurant employees’ coping behaviors: Do cash tips lead to more illicit behaviors?" The purpose of this research is to identify whether restaurant employees who receive cash at the end of each shift exhibit greater tendencies toward illicit behaviors than those who receive compensation in a weekly or bi-weekly paycheck. Using a mixed-method approach, this study will first examine which workplace stressors are the most impactful toward the need for employees to engage in illicit behaviors after a shift.
